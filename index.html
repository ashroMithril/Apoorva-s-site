I have worked on several projects, some as part of a team and others on my own:
  • Knowledge Accelerator: This is an ongoing project directed by Dr. Aniket Kittur at the Human-Computer Interaction Institute at Carnegie Mellon. The Knowledge Accelerator is a system that makes the crowd synthesize answers to questions asked by users using information present on the web. I worked closely with Dr. Kittur and Andrew Peters, the lead programmer of the project, on studying whether the quality of clips of text obtained from the crowd in one of the tasks in the system's workflow can be predicted using any known or calculable parameters, so as to guide refinements to the system. We developed a new task to obtain ratings for clips from the crowd and we ran the task on Amazon's Mechanical Turk to obtain ratings for several clips we had already gotten from the crowd for a test question. We used average clip rating as a measure of its crowd-perceived quality and studied how well it correlates with two clip parameters.

  • Get 'em Moles!, Bublz!: Get 'em Moles! & Bublz! are single-player games created in a team of 5 mentored by Dr. Amy Ogan & Erik Harpstead from Carnegie Mellon and Dr. Erin Walker from Arizona State University using the Unity game engine during a 15-day winter internship program in December 2013 at Bangalore, India.

  • Nash Equilibria for Road Networks: I developed a N-player strategic form game solver in Java and extended it to find the optimal distribution of traffic in simplistic road networks. I was mentored by Dr. Y. Narahari, Chairman, Department of Computer Science and Automation, Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore.

  • testsort: testsort is a script I wrote to perform a comparative analysis of the increase in the run-times of various sorting algorithms with increase in input size.

  • 32-bit Processor: A simple 32-bit processor that supports MIPS-like instructions. Written in Verilog. Link: g-karthik.github.io/32BitMIPSProcessor
